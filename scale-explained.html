Scale explained: 1:20, 1:50, 1:100 — and why it matters for your model

Scale is one of the first things architecture students learn and one of the most common things they get wrong in practice. A scale error discovered after cutting wastes material and time. Here is a clear explanation from the ground up.

What scale actually means

A scale ratio tells you how many times smaller your model or drawing is compared to the real thing. The format is always drawing size : real size.

1:20 means 1 unit in the drawing equals 20 units in reality.

So 1mm in the drawing = 20mm in reality.
Or: 1cm in the drawing = 20cm in reality.
Or: 1m in the drawing = 20m in reality — though you will never build a model that large.

The left number is always the drawing. The right number is always reality. A larger right number means a smaller drawing — 1:100 is smaller than 1:20.

Which scale to use for what

ScaleTypical useA 6m wall becomes
1:5Construction details — door handles, window profiles1200mm — impractical for a full model
1:10Detailed room sections, bathroom layouts600mm — large but workable
1:20Individual house or room models — most common for students300mm — fits on a table, easy to work with
1:50Apartment building, small commercial project120mm — compact, fine details are difficult
1:100Full site, large building60mm — too small to show interior detail
1:200Urban site plan, masterplan context30mm — symbolic only

Calculating dimensions — the formula

Model dimension = Real dimension ÷ Scale factor

The scale factor is the right-hand number in your ratio.

At 1:20:

  • 6000mm wall → 6000 ÷ 20 = 300mm
  • 3000mm wall → 3000 ÷ 20 = 150mm
  • 2700mm ceiling → 2700 ÷ 20 = 135mm
  • 900mm door → 900 ÷ 20 = 45mm
  • 111mm wall thickness → 111 ÷ 20 = 5.55mm (round to 6mm for laser cutting)

At 1:50:

  • 6000mm wall → 6000 ÷ 50 = 120mm
  • 3000mm wall → 3000 ÷ 50 = 60mm
  • 2700mm ceiling → 2700 ÷ 50 = 54mm

Common scale mistakes

Scaling twice. If you apply 1:20 scale in SketchUp during DXF export, then scale again in Illustrator, your pieces will be 400 times too small. Apply scale once — in SketchUp — and import at 1:1 in Illustrator.
Mixing units. SketchUp models are built in millimetres internally, even when the interface shows metres. When you draw a 3m wall, type 3000 in the dimension box — not 3. If you type 3, you have drawn a 3mm wall.
Not verifying before cutting. Always measure one known dimension in Illustrator with the ruler tool after importing your DXF. Confirm it matches your expected scaled dimension. This takes 30 seconds and prevents expensive material waste.

Scale on drawings vs scale on physical models

The word "scale" is used in two related but distinct ways:

  • On a 2D drawing — a floor plan printed at 1:50 means the building layout is reduced 50× on paper. You can read dimensions with a scale ruler.
  • On a physical model — a model built at 1:20 means every wall, every door, every window is physically one twentieth of real size. A 3mm sheet of plywood represents a 60mm structural element in reality.

They use the same ratios and the same maths — but they exist in different forms. A physical model at 1:20 looks like a physical model. A drawing at 1:20 looks like a drawing. Both are valid representations of the same design.
